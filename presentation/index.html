Can the Robots Save Peer Review?

Noise, bias, and the need for experimentation

Lukas Wallrich
Birkbeck Business School / FORRT · OSR 2026

The Reviewer Lottery

"About half of the papers accepted by one committee would have been rejected by the other."

— , NeurIPS 2021 Consistency Experiment

Meta-analysis of 48 studies: inter-rater reliability ICC = 0.34

The NeurIPS organisers assigned 10% of submissions to two independent committees — separate reviewers, separate area chairs. The two committees disagreed on 23% of papers. About half the accepted paper list would change if you simply re-rolled the dice. And this isn't an outlier. Bornmann et al.'s meta-analysis across 48 studies found a mean ICC of 0.34 and a kappa of 0.17. That's the system we trust to allocate careers and funding. And the system is under growing pressure. If AI helps all of us produce more papers — and it does — we'll break a publishing system that relies on volunteer peer review. The volume problem makes improving review quality more urgent, not less. LLMs can help with this — but the picture is more complicated than it first appears.

LLMs: A way to reduce noise

Human Review

  • ICC ≈ 0.34
  • Varies by mood, expertise, workload
  • No two reviewers evaluate the same dimensions, thus arbitrary coverage

LLM Review

  • Same paper → highly similar assessment
  • LLM overlap with humans: GPT-4 ≈ human–human overlap
  • ICLR trial: where reviewers used LLM feedback, quality improved 89% of the time
LLMs give you reproducibility. The ICLR 2025 trial was the first large-scale randomised experiment: 22,000 reviews, half received LLM feedback. 27% of reviewers updated their reviews, and quality improved in 89% of cases. Liang et al. in NEJM AI showed that the overlap between GPT-4 and human reviewers is comparable to the overlap between two human reviewers — about 30-39%. And let's be honest about the baseline we're comparing against. You agreed to review a paper in a weak moment. The deadline was two weeks ago. You finally open the manuscript on a Sunday evening, and it's adjacent to your expertise but not quite your area. That's the realistic scenario — not two deep experts carefully weighing every claim. In that context, an LLM as a cross-check adds real value. So on noise reduction, the case is strong. But consistency isn't everything...

Both Systems Are Biased

Human Bias

  • Prestige: top institutions had 1.4–1.7× advantage; blinding cuts to 1.0–1.3×
  • Geography: reviewers favour own-country authors; blinding doesn't fix it
  • Distributed → hard to detect or correct

LLM Bias

  • Also show prestige bias: slightly more rejections for low-prestige affiliations
  • But: consistent → in principle detectable and correctable
  • "In principle" matters — detection in small, heterogeneous datasets remains hard
Before we panic about LLM bias, let's look at what we already tolerate. The eLife study: when reviews were unblinded, top institutions had a 1.4 to 1.7 times advantage. Blinding cut that substantially — but we only know this because someone ran the experiment. Geography: Dumlao and Teplitskiy analysed 200,000 submissions across 60 STEM journals. Reviewers favour authors from their own country — and blinding doesn't fix it. LLMs have prestige bias too — Howell et al. found a slight increase in rejection for low-prestige affiliations. But here's the structural difference: algorithmic bias is consistent, so in principle you can audit for it and correct it. Human bias is distributed across thousands of individuals, making it nearly invisible. I say "in principle" deliberately — detecting bias in small, heterogeneous datasets is still genuinely hard. But at least the problem is tractable.

Beyond Bias:
Convergence & Surrender

Convergence

AI-assisted work: individually better, collectively more similar

AI use: output up 3×, but topic diversity down 4.6%

Cognitive Surrender

Users defer to AI output, reducing independent reasoning

If the LLM already said it, why think harder?

Not bugs to patch — human-AI interaction challenges that need training and discipline

This is what I worry about more than bias. Doshi and Hauser showed in Science Advances that AI-assisted creative work is individually better — but collectively more similar. Everyone converges on the same "good" answer. Hao et al. in Nature analysed 41 million papers. AI-augmented researchers publish three times more, get nearly five times the citations — but the total range of scientific topics contracted by 4.6%, and researchers engage across fewer areas. Cognitive surrender — Shaw and Nave's term — is the tendency to defer to AI output and reduce independent reasoning. In peer review specifically: Russo et al. found that at ICLR 2024, about 16% of reviews were AI-assisted. Those reviews inflated scores — borderline papers were nearly 5 percentage points more likely to be accepted when they happened to get an AI-assisted reviewer. These aren't technical bugs we can patch with better prompts. They're fundamental challenges of human-AI interaction. Convergence, anchoring, cognitive surrender — these are skills that scientists need to develop discipline around, whether they're reviewing papers, writing code, or generating hypotheses. The peer review context just makes the stakes particularly visible.

So ... Can Robots Save Peer Review?

For

  • Noise reduction at scale
  • Bias potentially auditable
  • Feedback quality comparable to humans

Against

  • Intellectual monoculture
  • Cognitive surrender
  • Detection of bias harder than it sounds

So, depends on how we integrate them —
and whether we develop the critical skills to use them well

So can the robots save peer review? The honest answer is: it depends on us. On the plus side: noise reduction, potentially auditable bias, and review quality that's comparable to the human baseline. On the minus side: convergence toward a narrower range of "acceptable" science, score inflation from cognitive surrender, and bias detection that's harder in practice than in theory. The answer isn't about the technology — it's about how we integrate it and whether we develop the critical skills to use it well. These are the same skills we need for every AI-augmented task: maintaining independent judgment, resisting anchoring, preserving diversity of thought.

What We Need

1. Experiment: Randomised trials, not position papers. ICLR showed it's possible at scale.

 2. Triangulate: Human–LLM disagreement as signal, not noise. Might each audit the other?

 3. Open up: Bans push use underground. Disclosure and transparency enables learning.

Three things we need. First, experiments. ICLR showed it's possible — 22,000 reviews, randomised, measured. We should make this standard, not exceptional. AAAI 2026 has started a pilot programme. More venues need to follow. Second, triangulation. When a human and an LLM disagree about a paper, that's not a problem — it's information. Each system can audit the other's blind spots. Third, transparency. Blanket prohibitions, as currently practised by many journals, push LLM use underground. That makes genuine comparison and mutual learning impossible. We need open experiments, not bans. A practical note on privacy: yes, feeding unpublished manuscripts into cloud-based LLMs raises confidentiality concerns. But with the rise of preprints, this is increasingly moot for many papers. And it's a solvable problem — local models, institutional APIs with data agreements, or simply limiting LLM use to preprinted work. It shouldn't be the reason we avoid the conversation entirely.
